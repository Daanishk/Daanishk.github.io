Computer Graphics // The Reversal

The Blob That Ate
the Neural Network

Three years ago, four researchers in France made a contrarian bet against the entire AI consensus. Now their fuzzy 3D blobs are running the Olympics, Hollywood, and your phone.

By Daanish Khan 12 min read Issue 34.04

In August 2023, four researchers published a fourteen-page paper at SIGGRAPH that did something the deep-learning community had spent five years insisting was impossible. Their method produced photoreal 3D scenes from ordinary photographs, in real time, on a single consumer GPU — without using a neural network as the scene model.

The technique is called 3D Gaussian Splatting. By early 2026 it has become the default approach to high-fidelity 3D capture: it's shipping in Hollywood VFX pipelines, Olympic broadcasts, real-estate platforms, and the universal 3D file formats used by Pixar, Apple, and NVIDIA. A follow-up paper this year, FastGS, has dragged training time from forty minutes down to one hundred seconds. One industry analyst recently described the moment as the JPEG moment for spatial computing — the point at which a piece of plumbing becomes ubiquitous enough to disappear into the background.

The original paper, by Bernhard Kerbl, Georgios Kopanas, Thomas Leimkühler, and George Drettakis of Inria and the Max Planck Institute, came out of nowhere. This is the story of how it works, why it caught the field off guard, and what it has built in the three years since.

§ 01 // The ProblemPhotographs in, 3D world out

The technical name is novel view synthesis. The pitch is simpler: take a few dozen photos of a place, get back a fully 3D version of it that you can fly through from any angle. A garden, a film set, an archaeological dig, an apartment listing. Until recently, this was hard.

The hard part isn't the photos — modern phones have plenty of resolution. The hard part is reconstructing the 3D structure that lies behind them: where every surface is, how it reflects light, what's behind what, what fills the gaps the camera never saw. Traditional photogrammetry — the technology behind Google Earth's 3D buildings — builds polygonal meshes, flat triangles glued together to approximate surfaces. Meshes render fast, but they're bad at the things meshes are bad at: hair, foliage, smoke, glass, anything fuzzy or semi-transparent. They also need to be told that an object exists. Cars and buildings get reconstructed; a leaf trembling on a branch usually does not.

In 2020, a team at UC Berkeley proposed a radically different approach.

§ 02 // The IncumbentNeRFs, and what they cost

Their idea was the Neural Radiance Field, or NeRF, and it was almost belligerently in tune with the spirit of the AI moment. Instead of building an explicit 3D model, you trained a small neural network to memorize the scene. The network's only job was to answer one question: given a point in space and a viewing direction, what color is it, and how transparent? To render a new view, you fired thousands of imaginary rays through each pixel of your virtual camera and asked the network to evaluate the color along each one. Sum the answers. There's your image.

NeRFs worked beautifully. They captured fuzz, transparency, and view-dependent reflections that meshes could not touch. They also took up to forty-eight hours to train a single scene and rendered the result at roughly fourteen seconds per frame. The reigning champion in 2023, a Google method called Mip-NeRF360, produced photoreal stills that were essentially impossible to navigate interactively.

The community spent the next three years racing to make NeRFs faster. InstantNGP, from NVIDIA, used a hash table to skip much of the neural-network cost. Plenoxels, from Berkeley, swapped the network out for a sparse voxel grid. Both got training down to minutes and rendering up to about ten frames per second — faster, but still nowhere near the thirty-frame threshold for real-time interaction at 1080p, and at the cost of visual quality.

The Inria team's response was to ask whether the neural network needed to be there at all.

§ 03 // How It WorksA scene as a swarm of blobs

Their replacement is conceptually simple. A scene is just a cloud of fuzzy, semi-transparent, stretched 3D blobs — Gaussians — each carrying a few stored properties:

Position in 3D space. Shape, expressed as an ellipsoid that can rotate and stretch along each axis. Color, with view-dependent variation so reflections work. And opacity, controlling how solid or transparent the blob is. To render a new view, the algorithm projects each blob onto the screen, sorts them by depth, and blends them back-to-front. There are no neural network evaluations during rendering. The whole thing is just drawing fuzzy ovals in the right order.

The pipeline starts with the same input as a NeRF: a set of photos and the camera positions from which they were taken (recovered automatically by Structure-from-Motion software, the same algorithm that powers photogrammetry). Instead of training a neural network on these inputs, the algorithm uses the sparse 3D points that fall out of the SfM step — usually a few thousand — as initial blob positions.

What follows is an optimization loop. The algorithm renders the current set of blobs from one of the training viewpoints, compares the result to the actual photo, and uses the error to nudge every blob's position, shape, color, and opacity in the direction that would have made the image more correct. The whole rendering pipeline is differentiable, which is what makes this work — gradients can be backpropagated through the splatting step the same way they would be through a neural network layer.

The Gaussians are anisotropic, meaning they can stretch into long, thin shapes to align with surfaces. This is one of the paper's quiet contributions. Earlier point-based rendering methods used spherical or disc-shaped primitives; the move to stretched ellipsoids lets the algorithm represent fine geometry — branches, wires, edges — with a small fraction of the primitives.

Every hundred iterations, the algorithm grooms its swarm. Blobs whose gradient signals tell them they're in an under-reconstructed region — a piece of geometry that needs more detail — get cloned: a copy is made and nudged in the direction of greatest error. Blobs that are too large for the detail they're trying to cover get split into two smaller ones. Blobs that have faded to near-transparent through optimization get culled. The swarm starts at a few thousand and grows to between one and five million by the end of training, depending on scene complexity. The whole thing is less like training a neural network and more like a sculptor adding clay where it's needed and chiseling it away where it's not.

The final piece is the rasterizer: a custom GPU program written in CUDA that does the actual splatting. The screen is divided into 16×16 pixel tiles. For each frame, the GPU sorts every blob exactly once by depth (using radix sort, which is what GPUs are good at), then assigns each tile a list of the blobs that overlap it. Each tile is processed in parallel, blending its blobs front-to-back until the pixels saturate. This is the part that makes the whole thing real-time. The rasterizer is also fully differentiable, so gradients flow back through it during training.

§ 04 // Why It's FastThe hardware was always ready

Two things, really.

First, GPUs are built to do exactly this. Drawing millions of simple shapes — triangles, sprites, blobs — fast and in parallel is the literal job description of every consumer GPU from the last twenty years of video games. Neural networks fight this hardware: they're matrix-multiplication machines. Splatting plays to the hardware's natural rhythm.

Second, the explicit representation skips the empty-space problem. NeRFs evaluate their network at hundreds of points along every ray, including all the points that turn out to be empty air. Splatting only touches the blobs that exist. In a typical outdoor scene, the optimization concentrates blobs on actual surfaces and leaves the empty volumes empty. There's nothing to evaluate where there's nothing there.

The team didn't reject machine learning. They rejected the idea that the scene itself needed to be a neural network. The blobs are just blobs. The optimization is what's smart.

The numbers from the original paper are stark. On the same scenes and hardware, Mip-NeRF360 takes forty-eight hours to train and renders at 0.07 frames per second. The Inria method matches its quality after about forty-one minutes of training and renders at 134 frames per second. That's roughly seventy times faster training and nineteen hundred times faster rendering — and slightly better visual quality, by the standard image-similarity metrics.

§ 05 // Now ShippingWhere the splats actually live

What was a research result in August 2023 is now infrastructure. A non-exhaustive tour of the past eighteen months:

Film & VFX
Hollywood pipelines The Foundry's Nuke 17.0 — the compositing software that runs the visual-effects pipeline of nearly every major Hollywood production — added native Gaussian splat support in late 2025. OTOY's OctaneRender now does full path-traced splat rendering. Superman (2025) was the first major motion picture to ship with dynamic splats baked into its production pipeline, used for environment captures that were re-lit and re-framed in post.
Live Broadcast
The 2026 Milan Olympics The Games used splat-based virtual cameras for ski jumping, hockey, and figure skating broadcasts — putting viewers in vantage points no human cameraman could occupy. The captures happen in near-real-time during the event, then get streamed back into the broadcast feed.
Real Estate
Smartphone walkthroughs Zillow rolled out splat-based home tours through its SkyTours product. Apartments.com followed with exterior splat support via Matterport. The capture rig that used to require a $4,000 360-camera setup is now an agent walking through the house with a phone.
Mobile & AR
Splats in your pocket Mobile-GS, presented at ICLR 2026, runs full splat rendering on consumer phones at interactive framerates. Apple's Vision Pro got native splat support through the MetalSplatter library earlier this year. Apple's own SHARP system, also released this year, generates a complete 3D splat scene from a single photograph — meaning historical photos and product catalogs become explorable spaces.
Standardization
The quietly consequential one In February 2026, the Khronos Group — the consortium behind OpenGL and Vulkan — added KHR_gaussian_splatting as an extension to glTF 2.0, the universal 3D file format for the web. In March, the Alliance for OpenUSD (led by Pixar, Apple, NVIDIA, and Adobe) added Gaussian splats as a first-class type in OpenUSD, the format used across film, simulation, and digital twins. Once standardized, splats stop being a separate ecosystem and start being interoperable with every existing 3D pipeline.
4D Capture
The next frontier: time Several follow-up papers extend the technique to dynamic scenes — capturing not just spaces but performances, sports plays, surgical procedures. World Labs, which raised a billion dollars in 2025, ships a system called Marble that generates entire 3D environments from text prompts and exports them as splats.

§ 06 // The Larger PointWhat the blobs reveal

It is tempting to read this story as "old beats new" and move on. That misses the actual lesson.

The Inria team didn't reject machine learning. They kept gradient descent, differentiable rendering, and the entire modern optimization toolkit. What they rejected was the assumption that the scene itself needed to be a neural network. The blobs are just blobs. The optimization is what's smart. The neural network was load-bearing for one specific job — interpolating colors along rays — and once you removed it and replaced it with explicit primitives that GPUs could draw natively, everything got faster, sharper, and smaller.

In a moment when the answer to most computing questions has become "add more parameters," that's a useful counterexample to keep in mind. The largest practical leap in 3D rendering since NeRFs came not from a bigger neural network but from someone willing to ask whether the neural network needed to be there at all.
